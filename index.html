Editorial Philosophy

Impactful editing begins with comprehension. To improve a piece of writing, an editor must first understand it—we must be able to trace the piece's argument from premise to conclusion, following the steps of logic and incorporating evidence. This standard holds true regardless of if we work through a financial analysis, a regulatory submission, or an investor letter: If the reasoning does not gel with us, neither will it gel with a reader. My job as an editor is to ensure that the complexity of your project is presented with the clarity and precision it needs.

I believe editing is a collaborative act between authors and subject matter experts, who have deep expertise in what they are trying to express, and editors with the expertise in how to communicate it. These two things complement one another. If I'm having trouble with a particular passage, I lead by asking questions. To me, this is saying X. Is this understanding correct? or Earlier we established Y, and now we're saying Z—are these two things contradictory, or am I missing the connection? This approach keeps the author's knowledge at the center of the work while addressing genuine opportunities for misunderstanding before they become problems on the page.

Editorial decisions don't begin at the editing phase, but at the moment a piece of work is conceived. Choosing the right modality for the desired message, understanding the target audience, and outlining the development process are critical decisions that affect the time spent editing later. Well-designed workflows and editorial tools (e.g., templates, style guides) create a strong foundation for authors to build from and build organizational capacity. This ensures that quality becomes a shared standard throughout the organization rather than a limitation to be suffered later. I find creating these tools to be just as satisfying as the editing process; a well-designed modality for visual presentation complements the quality of the piece itself.
